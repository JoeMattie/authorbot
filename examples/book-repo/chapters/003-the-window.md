Once you stop blaming an instrument, you have to start listening to it.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019d7c34-ac40-7cb2-86e8-0634a0f8f138" -->
They raised the sampling rate until the seventy-one-minute arc opened like a slowed film, and inside it there was structure: a rise, a plateau, a stutter of eleven evenly spaced notches, a fall. It repeated without variation, cycle after cycle.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019d7c35-96a0-7c5a-8431-511f89a421d2" -->
> Run 52, 03:05. The notches are not noise. Eleven of them, every cycle, spacing constant to the nanosecond. It reads less like a heartbeat now and more like a shutter. Something is opening, and I do not know which side of it we are on. -- M.V.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019d7c36-8100-7526-98a1-595c3929e8f0" -->
Theo called the shape a window function out of professional habit, then heard himself say it and went quiet.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019d7c37-6b60-70e4-99f7-cb8159b54f71" -->
A window implies a wall. It implies an aperture cut on purpose, and a purpose implies an audience, and neither of them said the next sentence out loud.
