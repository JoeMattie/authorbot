<!-- authorbot:block id="019cadfe-7360-7049-a30b-1f5898a5020a" -->
The drift appeared on a Tuesday, in the fourth decimal place, where nobody respectable ever looks.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019cadff-5dc0-7486-a457-a15be68bb8eb" -->
Mara Voss found it because the nightly calibration of the deep-field interferometer was her favorite hour at Hollow Creek: the annex quiet, the vacuum pumps humming their one note, the residual plot flattening into the clean line that meant the universe was behaving.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019cae00-4820-7bef-81b9-c45c817eee7f" -->
The line was not clean. Every seventy-one minutes the residuals bowed upward in the same small arc, the same amplitude, the same shape, like a heartbeat traced by someone very patient and very far away.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019cae01-3280-79cf-95fd-0902938bded2" -->
She did what trained people do. She suspected the instrument, then the mounting, then the building, then herself, and she logged every suspicion with a timestamp so that future embarrassment would at least be well organized.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019cae02-1ce0-7f02-b609-9f3e2e9c2636" -->
By morning she had a folder named BASELINE-DRIFT and a decision to say nothing yet, because naming a thing is the first admission that it exists.
